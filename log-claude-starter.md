Bringing in @Madhavan Ramanujam and @Elena Verna for practical perspectives.

Pricing a new product comes down to four decisions — get them in this order:

**1. Who is your customer?** Segment by willingness to pay, not demographics. A startup and an enterprise may use the same product very differently.

**2. What value are you delivering?** Identify the *one* metric that best captures the value delivered — usage, seats, revenue processed, outcomes. This becomes your pricing metric.

**3. What is your monetization model?** Options: subscription, usage-based, outcome-based, freemium + paid tiers, one-time. Match the model to how value is *experienced* over time.

**4. What's the price level?** Run willingness-to-pay research — Van Westendorp (4-question survey) for early signal, conjoint analysis for more precision. Do NOT anchor to costs or competitors first. Anchor to value.

**Common mistake:** Pricing on cost-plus or just copying a competitor. That leaves massive revenue on the table. Always start with value, then pressure-test against alternatives.

From a growth lens, your pricing model is also your *acquisition strategy* — they must be aligned.

**If you choose product-led growth (PLG):** You need a free tier or trial that lets users experience value before paying. Your pricing metric should be usage-based so it scales naturally with the user's success. Freemium works when the free tier creates habits and the paid tier removes friction at the moment of value.

**If you choose sales-led growth (SLG):** Simpler tier structure, annual contracts, and price anchoring on ROI matter more. You can price higher with lower transparency.

**Hybrid (PLG + SLG):** Start with self-serve pricing visible on your site, then layer in enterprise/custom pricing for larger accounts. This is the dominant model for B2B SaaS in 2024.

**Key tactic:** Launch with 2–3 tiers max. Too many options cause paralysis. Use the middle tier as your anchor — most customers will pick it, and it should represent your best margin.

**One thing I'd stress:** Ship your pricing fast, with a hypothesis, then iterate based on conversion data. Pricing is not permanent — the cost of being wrong is lower than the cost of delaying.
